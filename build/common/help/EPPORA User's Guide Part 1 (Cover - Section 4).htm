1.    Overview

1.1    EPPORA

With projects failing at an alarming rate, it is imperative that organizations be very conscious of (1) selecting which projects to implement, (2) determining project costs not only at the start of projects but also when the project scope (requirements) changes, and (3) allocating their precious labor resources across active projects to assure maximum ROI for their project investment. The purpose of EPPORA (Enterprise Project Portfolio Optimized Resource Allocation) is to provide a system that allows users to scientifically determine which projects to implement, accurately determine project cost at the project’s start and during its lifecycle and determine how to optimally allocate precious labor resources across projects to assure maximum ROI for an organization’s project portfolio.  EPPORA’s ability to achieve these three critical project activities is predicated upon the following premises:

 

1.     Requirements are the foundation of projects.

2.     A key to a successful project is to specify requirements that are uniquely identifiable, understood the same by all project team members and stakeholders, are testable and are traceable.

3.     It is easier to accurately estimate smaller project tasks that are less than six workdays of effort than it is to estimate project tasks that take considerably longer than five workdays.

4.     It is difficult, if not next to impossible, to have Project Managers do significantly extra work to improve their estimates, especially during the project’s lifecycle.

5.     It is commonly recognized by project management professionals that schedules are an integral part of successfully managing a project and need to be continuously updated throughout the project’s lifecycle.

6.     It is critical to assign an organization’s finest labor resources to its most important projects’ tasks to achieve maximum ROI for the organization’s project portfolio.

The initial step in EPPORA is providing a scientific method for selecting which projects shall be funded.  How organizations select their projects is a major factor in determining their competitive advantages.  Built-in to EPPORA are a set of project selection criteria such as the project’s cost, anticipated ROI, synergy with other projects, conformance to government regulations, alignment with organizational goals and strategies.  Each has a default importance factor (weight) and EPPORA Administrators and Project Portfolio Managers can modify each criterion’s importance and/or add additional criteria.  Rules are established for how each project’s criterion shall be evaluated and EPPORA helps automate the process.

 

To determine a project’s cost EPPORA links each low-level requirement (those without sub-requirements) to the appropriate project tasks needed for their implementation.  To summarize, the best project estimates do not take a high level view of a project’s cost, rather they are derived bottom up by breaking major tasks into smaller and smaller project tasks.  This facilitates obtaining a reasonably accurate estimate of both the duration and work effort in calculating a project task’s cost and allows individuals who are actually performing the project task to participate in the estimation effort.  It has the side benefit of making the project task’s implementer feel responsible for the project task’s ownership, which motivates the project team members to perform at their best in assuring that each project task is delivered on time and within budget.  Another key element of making accurate estimates is experiential data.  The more information one has at his/her fingertips when making an estimate, the greater the probability the estimate will be accurate.  This is the primary reason that project task cost estimates are far more accurate later in the project than earlier, since the estimators have more information as the project progresses. 

 

Future versions of EPPORA will take advantage of this by using an “Estimate Database” of completed project tasks for requirements and providing a search engine (synonym searches for like requirements and project tasks).  This will allow estimators to be able to look at what has happened in the past relative to estimates for similar required project tasks when making estimates about the future. More and more organizations recognize the critical link between the quality of their requirements and the success/failure of their projects.  To use a project’s requirements as a foundation for estimating a project’s cost, a new requirements specification method is needed that facilitates assuring that premises 1 and 2 above are valid. EPPORA accomplishes this by:

 

Ø  Providing a hierarchically structured method for expressing a project’s requirements.

Ø  Each requirement is understood the same by all members of the project team and stakeholders.

Ø  Uniquely identifying each requirement supporting comprehensive test cases verifying each requirement.

Ø  Including a “Requirements Analyzer” component as an integral part of EPPORA to assure that each requirement is satisfies an organization’s standards.

The last key feature of EPPORA, the optimal allocation of an organization’s precious resources to its ongoing project’s tasks requires the initial two key features to optimize the allocation.  EPPORA assures that the most important project tasks receive the finest resources available while also considering current project experience, logistics, costs, etc.  It accomplishes in minutes what would take a team of Project Managers weeks to achieve.  Thus with an ever changing project environment, EPPORA provides an organization timely solutions to some very complex problems on a daily basis.  In addition, future versions of EPPORA will support modeling that enables Executives, Project Portfolio Managers and Business Analysts to run “what-if” exercises in order to see what would happen to cost and schedule if the resources and/or requirements were changed.

 

The EPPORA pages (often called screens or windows in other applications) described in sections 6 through 9 provide consistency in terms of button layout across all EPPORA pages.  Thus the buttons “Update,” “Delete,” “Create” and “Cancel” always appear in that order from left to right on the page.

4.1.6   Project Team Members

EPPORA Project Team Members do not have any major responsibilities within the EPPORA system. The purpose of having a Project Team Member user class is for EPPORA to be able to automatically assign actual labor resources for generic labor resources to tasks during the allocation period.  The EPPORA Project Team Members report to their respective Project Managers. Their responsibility is to update their attributes whenever a change occurs, such as a new email address, and perform their assigned project tasks in a timely manner.

4.2       Generic Labor Categories

An important concept in EPPORA is that all low-level project tasks are initially assigned generic labor resources instead of actual staff members.  When a project task becomes ready for execution (allocation period, see Section 4.12) then an actual Project Team Member is assigned responsibility for the project’s task implementation. An organization can have as many “Labor Categories” as needed. The following are examples:

 

Software Engineer

Test Engineer

Programmer Level 1

Programmer Level 2

Project Management Office Director

Configuration Manager

 

 

 

Each “Labor Category” can have as many levels as needed.  Each Project Team Member is assigned to one or more generic labor categories and has an assigned hourly salary.  An average hourly salary for each labor category is automatically computed by EPPORA and continuously adjusted as project tasks are completed.  Each generic labor category average hourly salary is determined by multiplying the actual hours expended by individuals who belong to the generic labor category times their hourly salary, adding the results of all the individuals belonging to that generic labor category and then dividing by the total number of hours expended (see Section 4.9 for a more detailed explanation). 

4.3    Parent-Child Task Relationship

EPPORA makes use of a concept much like the parent-child relationship that exists in our society for the tasks within project schedules and requirements.  A parent schedule task consists of multiple subtasks.  A major characteristic of a parent schedule task is that it does not directly have any resources assigned to it.  Nor is it dependent upon other tasks.  Children tasks that are themselves not parents can be dependent upon other tasks and must have generic labor resources assigned.  They can also have inventory resources (supplies, equipment, etc.) assigned along with “Other” expenses (travel, per diem, etc.). EPPORA automatically calculates the following attributes of a parent task:

Ø  Start Date (automatically calculated from children tasks)

Ø  Finish Date (automatically calculated from children tasks)

Ø  Level of Effort (automatically calculated from accumulation of low-level children tasks “Level of Effort”)

Ø  Cost (automatically calculated from sum of labor cost plus inventory and other cost)

Ø  % Complete (automatically calculated from “Level of Effort” and “Work Accomplished”)

Ø  Flags (automatically determined from task’s conditions)

4.4    Requirements Matrix

The cornerstone of any project is its requirements. EPPORA uses a “Structured Tabular” method for specifying requirements (hereinafter called “Requirements Matrix”).  The “Structured Tabular” method helps assure that each requirement is unique, understood the same by all project team members and stakeholders and can readily be tested and traced to other critical project documents.  The following example shows the requirements for specifying Microsoft Word’s “Table Insert” command (most computer users are familiar with the functionality of this command). With the “Outline” method (what is commonly used to express requirements), one would expect to see the requirements specification of the “Table Insert Command” as:

 

Users shall be able to insert columns, rows and/or cells into a table prior, after, above or below an existing column, row or cell. 

 

The following example illustrates the “Structured Tabular” format for specifying the requirements for Microsoft Word’s “Table Insert” command using EPS’s “Requirement Matrix” method.  The technical barriers to be resolved are: (1) creating, designing and implementing a new method for specifying requirements and a set of complementary software tools that resolves requirements problems to the greatest extent possible and (2) assuring that the requirement testing is as comprehensive as possible.  The following example shows the requirements for specifying Microsoft Word’s Table Insert command using the “Requirements Matrix” method. The difference is subtle, but critical in assuring meaningful requirements. Each “Requirement ID” can have a level from 0 to 1 that is greater than the preceding level.  If the following “Requirement ID” is greater than the current, then the current requirement is considered a “Parent Requirement ID” and is only used for clarification purposes.

The 1,000 maximum for rows and columns and the 10,000 for cells are just numbers selected for illustration purposes.  Each requirement in the EPPORA method can have as many test cases as needed (T01 through Tnn).  Each test becomes uniquely identifiable using the format of “Req’t-ID—Test ID.” The ID’s make it easier to visualize what tests are missing, calculate the percentage of test coverage and obtain a clearer understanding of how the product will be verified. 

The Test Procedures provide the detailed step-by-step description of how each test will be run, the success criteria, test responsibility, etc.  The “Requirements Matrix” method allows non-technical project stakeholders the capability of determining the comprehensiveness of the testing activity.  Additionally, the automated tools of EPPORA (“Requirements Analyzer) help to ensure that each requirement is unique and understood the same by all Project Members and stakeholders.  EPPORA has simplified a critical activity within a project, the verification of a deliverable satisfying its requirements.  Part of the development team’s tasks is to create a set of test cases to ensure that each deliverable’s requirement can be verified prior to product acceptance. It should be noted that each deliverable can have any number of requirements. 

Likewise, the same requirement can appear for multiple deliverables. One of the major advantages of specifying the “Requirements Matrix” format is that it makes it very clear what tests need to be built to fully verify each requirement.  The test case numbers, T01-Tnn identify each test as well as the specific requirement being verified.  Each requirement can have as many tests as necessary to verify that the product satisfies the requirement.  The “Requirements Matrix” method makes it far simpler to visually determine if the tests provided are comprehensive and/or what information is missing.  Without using EPS’s “Requirements Matrix” method, what most organizations end up with relative to their requirements can be summarized with the immortal words from the movie “Cool Hand Luke,” “What we have here is a failure to communicate?” Fortunately, the use of EPPORA’s “Requirements Matrix” method helps an organization eliminate communication dilemmas.

4.5    Triggers

EPPORA automatically monitors a set of possible conditions whose purpose is to warn key individuals of events that might affect the bottom line or a project’s schedule, cost or quality.  These events, when they occur, are called “triggers” and they cause EPPORA to send out a set of emails to designated individuals notifying them of the event’s occurrence.

4.6    Supervisors

Each staff member except those with a labor category of CEO has an assigned supervisor.  An assigned supervisor is not a user type.  It is used to notify the supervisor for individuals that they are responsible for when events occur (triggers, see Section 4.8), which may affect a project’s success, such as approval of vacations.

4.7    Sponsors

Each project has an assigned sponsor.  A sponsor is not a user type in that it does not imply any access rights.  It is used merely to notify the project sponsor of critical events for his/her projects (triggers, see Section 4.8).

4.8    EOB (End of Business)

At the end of each business day EPPORA automatically performs a set of operations to verify that there are no critical issues that warrant an action from a Project Manager, Project Portfolio Manager or Executive.  When one of these events does occur, it is called a trigger (see Section 4.8).  EPPORA commences making its checks at the designated end of business time (defaulted to 3 AM).  An Administrator can change the default time.

 

 

4.9    Average Hourly Salary

In determining the average hourly salary for each labor category, EPPORA uses a weighted average that takes into consideration the number of hours each member of a labor category has worked for the current year.  The formula is:

Average Hourly Labor Category Salaryi =

(∑(Staff Member for Labor Category Hours i) * (Salaryi))/Total Hours Expended in Labor Categoryi)

For example, if an employee named Jack works 999 hours and makes $1 per hour and an employee named Shirley works 1 hour and makes $1,001 per hour, one might say the average hourly salary is $501 using the formula:

Average Hourly Labor Categoryi Salary   =

 ((Jack’s Hourly Salary) + (Shirley’s Hourly Salary)) / (Number in Labor Category)

            = ($1+$1,001) / (2) = $501

Whereas EPPORA calculates it as:

Average Hourly Labor Category Salaryi =

 

((Jack’s Hourly Salary)*(Number Hours Worked in Labor Category)) +

((Shirley’s Hourly Salary)*(Number Hours Worked in Labor Category)) /

Number Hours Expended This Year in Labor Category)

 

= ($1*999) + ($1,001*1) / (1,000) = $2,000/1,000 = $2

The weighted “Average Hourly Salary” used by EPPORA provides a clearer picture of the average hourly salary paid for the labor category.  This value is used by EPPORA in estimating the cost of a project and in calculating the cost effectiveness of each project team member for each labor category as they perform project tasks during the year.  At the end of each year an artificial employee is created with 100 hours for each labor category.  If the hours expended for a labor category is less than 100, then the lesser number is used.  The New Year begins relatively fresh using a small base for each labor category so each project team member’s cost effectiveness starts fresh annually.  The 100 hours is the default and can be changed by an Administrator.

4.10  Productivity Factor

A key factor in an organization’s present and future success is how productive its staff is performing their jobs.  Theoretically, an individual’s productivity factor is defined by how efficiently (speed and quality) he/she is able to complete each assignment.  Productivity should be one of the major factors in evaluating an individual’s value to the organization and his/her compensation. Of course there are other factors such as synergy (does the individual improve the performance of other individuals), leadership and the ability to perform complex tasks. In almost every organization financial rewards such as compensation and bonuses are left to the discretion of a chain of command from the individual’s boss to several levels of management to the Chief Executive Officer or President. EPPORA removes a significant percentage of subjective decision making in the process by scientifically keeping track of how well each individual performs in each of his/her labor categories.  EPPORA does this by:

Ø  Having Project Managers create schedules using generic labor categories for each task and assuming an average performer in each category when estimating the level of effort for each task.

Ø  If the actual individual assigned to a task performs a task in the estimated effort, he/she is given a productivity factor of “1.0” for that task.

Ø  If an actual individual assigned to a task performs a task in twice the estimated effort, he/she is given a productivity factor of “.5” for that task.

Ø  If an actual individual assigned to a task performs a task in half the estimated effort, he/she is given a productivity factor of “2.0” for that task.

Ø  Throughout the year for each completed task for each individual, EPPORA adjusts their productivity factor for each of their labor categories.

4.11  Cost Effectiveness

One would hope that if one individual in a labor category makes $2 per hour and another individual is making $1 per hour, that the individual making $2 per hour is more productive, like twice as productive, as the individual making $1 per hour.  EPPORA determines an individual’s cost effectiveness by:

Ø  Calculating the average hourly salary for all individuals belonging to a labor category (on a prorated basis using salary and hours expended).  This becomes the hourly salary for the average individual in the labor category.

Ø  Comparing the individual’s actual hourly salary and “Productivity Factor” for each task they performed during the year with the labor category’s average hourly salary.

4.12  Allocation Period

A unique feature of EPPORA is that it does not require allocation of actual labor resources for the entire project.  In real life, requirements, project resources, milestones and deliverable dates are always changing.  Allocating resources for projects with more than six months duration remaining is difficult enough with a work force that (1) never leaves the company, (2) is never sick (3) never takes an unexpected vacation, (4) never has to make a change to a requirement. 

EPPORA has Project Managers create their schedules using generic labor resources (labor categories), or (5) has no internal or external unexpected disruptions.  The default “Allocation Period” is three weeks, but can be changed by an Administrator.  The allocation process is automated by EPPORA.  It utilizes the ranking of projects within the portfolio and tasks within a project (critical path) to optimize the allocation.  It also considers previous assignments, logistics, project synergy, etc. in allocating the most productive resources to the most important projects.

4.13    Conditional Resource Modeling

When schedules are not tracking as expected, management often wishes to know what will happen to the schedule and costs if critical resources are added.  EPPORA second release will support the specification of additional resources to be used for a modeling exercise so that Executives and Project Portfolio Managers can better ascertain the results of adding or removing critical resources.

4.15    Criterion Weight

4.16    Near Optimal Resource Allocation Process

EPPORA automatically allocates labor resources to the tasks scheduled for the allocation period. It accomplishes the allocation in the following order:

  1. Allocates any pre-assigned resources to the designated tasks.
  2. It allocates any partially completed tasks to the previously assigned resources.
  3. It sorts all the remaining tasks based on priority, from most important to least important task.
  4. It sorts the individuals supporting the needed labor categories for the tasks within the allocation period in productivity order, from most productive to least productive.
  5. It assigns partially completed tasks to the individuals who previously worked on the tasks.
  6. It assigns the individuals designated by Project Portfolio Managers to their assigned tasks.
  7. It assigns the most productive individual within a labor category to the most important task needing the aforementioned labor category.
